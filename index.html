About Us

Hands and Looms of India is a social enterprise dedicated to reviving and reimagining India's traditional crafts by empowering artisans through design, innovation, and technology. Rooted in the spirit of Vocal for Local and aligned with Viksit Bharat 2047, we bridge the gap between heritage and modernity—connecting age-old craftsmanship with contemporary design education and global markets.

Heritage Preservation

Protecting and celebrating India's rich cultural heritage while ensuring traditional crafts remain relevant in the modern world.

Design Innovation

Merging traditional techniques with contemporary design thinking to create products that resonate with today's markets.

Global Markets

Connecting skilled artisans with national and international markets, ensuring fair compensation and sustainable growth.

Traditional Crafts We Protect

From ritual devotional panels to ceremonial embroidery, each craft carries generations of cultural wisdom and artistic excellence

Mata Ni Pachedi

Gujarat (Kathiawar & Saurashtra) - Ritual embroidered or painted devotional panels used in goddess worship, depicting local mythology and ritual narratives.

Toda Embroidery

Nilgiris, Tamil Nadu - Ceremonial shawls with distinctive red-and-black geometrical patterns integral to pastoral Toda community's social and ritual life.

Phulkari

Punjab - Traditional embroidery featuring vibrant floral patterns and intricate needlework, from domestic tradition to fashion runway.

Kantha

Bengal & Odisha - Running stitch embroidery traditionally used for quilting old saris and dhotis, now adapted for contemporary markets.

Chamba Rumal

Himachal Pradesh - Fine needlework with pictorial scenes depicting legends, religious themes, and court life in miniature painting style.

Kutch & Ajrakh

Gujarat - Mirror work embroidery (Kutch) and resist-dyed block printing (Ajrakh) representing rich nomadic and pastoral traditions.

Banaras Brocade

Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh - Luxurious silk brocade with gold and silver zari work, traditionally used for wedding and ceremonial wear.

Ikat Textiles

Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Northeast - Resist-dyeing technique where threads are tie-dyed before weaving, creating distinctive blurred patterns.

Key Initiatives

Transforming traditional craftsmanship through education, collaboration, and sustainable growth

Skill Development Hubs

Establish Hands and Looms Learning Centres in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities near artisan clusters. Provide training in illustration, Photoshop, CAD, digital marketing, and business basics.

Institutional Collaboration

Partner with NIFT, NID, IITs, and design schools. Enable students to intern with artisan communities to co-create modern, market-ready designs.

Mentorship & Evaluation

Organize biannual exhibitions and fairs to showcase artisan-student collaborations. Evaluate work based on innovation, design quality, and market response.

Entrepreneurship Support

Provide seed material, tools, and mentorship for outstanding artisans to start their own ventures. Facilitate micro-grants and sustainable business models.

Root Causes & Challenges

Understanding the systemic barriers that limit awareness and threaten the survival of traditional textile arts

Education & Exposure Gaps

Schools and popular media rarely teach textile histories or traditional techniques, leaving generations disconnected from their cultural heritage.

Weak Marketing & Provenance Systems

Lack of accessible certification or simple provenance tags makes it difficult for consumers to verify authenticity and understand craft origins.

Intermediary-Heavy Value Chains

Multiple middlemen between artisan and consumer mean buyers rarely meet makers or learn the stories behind each piece.

Cultural Specificity Barrier

Ritual textiles are context-bound and require storytelling to be legible to outsiders. Without narrative, they become mere decorative objects.

Economic Pressures

Younger community members leave craft practice for other livelihoods due to low and unstable incomes, reducing intergenerational knowledge transmission.

The Feedback Loop:

Less public awareness → Lower demand → Fewer apprenticeships → Reduced production → Even less public exposure
